🧲 Magnets P3DiversityInteractions

MOE Learning Objective: Identify magnetic and non-magnetic materials; understand magnetic poles and forces of attraction/repulsion.

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Test objects to identify magnetic materials (iron, steel, nickel, cobalt)
  • Identify the North and South poles of a magnet
  • Predict whether magnets will attract or repel
  • Give examples of how magnets are used in daily life in Singapore

🔹 What is a Magnet?

A magnet is an object that produces a magnetic field and can attract certain materials.

  • Magnets have two ends called poles: North (N) and South (S)
  • The magnetic force is strongest at the poles
  • Magnets can attract magnetic materials: iron, steel, nickel, cobalt
  • Materials like plastic, wood, glass, and aluminium are non-magnetic
Try This: Test objects around your HDB home with a magnet. Which ones stick?

🔹 Magnetic Forces: Attract and Repel

Like Poles Repel

When two North poles or two South poles face each other, they push away (repel).

Unlike Poles Attract

When a North pole faces a South pole, they pull together (attract).

Rule: "Like poles repel, unlike poles attract" — remember this for your Science tests!

🔹 Uses of Magnets in Singapore

  • MRT trains: Magnetic sensors ensure doors close safely
  • HDB kitchens: Refrigerator doors use magnetic seals to keep food fresh
  • Compasses: Used for orienteering at MacRitchie Reservoir and Pulau Ubin
  • Electronics: Speakers in your tablet or headphones contain magnets
  • Recycling: Magnets separate iron/steel from other waste at recycling centres
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • At the Science Centre Singapore, you can experiment with giant electromagnets that lift cars!
  • NParks uses magnetic tags to track animals in nature reserves for conservation.
  • Many Singaporean inventions, like magnetic levitation (MagLev) research at NTU, use magnetic principles.
💡Fun Fact: The Earth is a giant magnet! Its magnetic field protects us from harmful solar radiation and makes compasses work.

🫀 Human Digestive System P3Systems

MOE Learning Objective: Identify parts of the digestive system and describe their functions in breaking down food.

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Label the main parts: mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine
  • Describe the function of each part in digestion
  • Explain the path food takes through the body
  • Understand why digestion is important for growth and energy

🔹 What is Digestion?

Digestion is the process of breaking down food into small nutrients that your body can absorb and use for energy, growth, and repair.

  • Food enters through the mouth where teeth chew and saliva starts breaking it down
  • Food travels down the oesophagus (food pipe) to the stomach
  • In the stomach, acids and enzymes break food into a liquid mixture
  • The small intestine absorbs nutrients into the bloodstream
  • The large intestine absorbs water; waste leaves through the anus
Remember: Digestion takes about 24-72 hours from mouth to exit!

🔹 Functions of Each Organ

Mouth

  • Teeth cut, tear, and grind food
  • Saliva contains enzymes that start digesting starch
  • Tongue helps mix food and taste

Stomach

  • Muscular walls churn food
  • Gastric juice (acid + enzymes) breaks down proteins
  • Kills harmful bacteria in food

Small Intestine

  • Longest part (~6 meters in adults!)
  • Most digestion and nutrient absorption happens here
  • Has tiny finger-like projections called villi to increase surface area

Large Intestine

  • Absorbs water from undigested food
  • Forms faeces (waste)
  • Stores waste before removal
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • When you eat chicken rice at a hawker centre, your digestive system breaks down rice (carbohydrates), chicken (protein), and sauce (fats) into nutrients.
  • Health Promotion Board (HPB) teaches "My Healthy Plate" to help Singaporeans eat balanced meals that support good digestion.
  • Drinking enough water (recommended: 6-8 glasses/day in our hot climate) helps the large intestine function properly.
💡Fun Fact: Your small intestine is about 6 meters long — that's taller than a 2-storey HDB block! It's folded up inside your body to fit.

🦋 Life Cycles of Animals P4Cycles

MOE Learning Objective: Compare life cycles of animals with 3 stages (e.g., grasshopper) and 4 stages (e.g., butterfly).

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe the 4 stages of a butterfly: Egg → Larva → Pupa → Adult
  • Describe the 3 stages of a grasshopper: Egg → Nymph → Adult
  • Explain why young animals may look different from adults (metamorphosis)
  • Identify examples of animals with each type of life cycle in Singapore

🔹 What is a Life Cycle?

A life cycle is the series of changes an animal goes through from birth to adulthood and reproduction.

  • Complete metamorphosis (4 stages): Butterfly, beetle, mosquito, bee
  • Incomplete metamorphosis (3 stages): Grasshopper, cockroach, dragonfly, termite

🔹 Butterfly Life Cycle (4 Stages)

1. Egg

  • Laid on host plant (e.g., lime tree for Common Jay butterfly)
  • Tiny, often round or oval
  • Protects developing embryo

2. Larva (Caterpillar)

  • Hatches from egg
  • Eats leaves constantly to grow
  • Moults (sheds skin) several times
  • Looks very different from adult

3. Pupa (Chrysalis)

  • Caterpillar forms protective casing
  • Inside, body completely reorganises
  • Does not eat or move much
  • Takes 1-2 weeks in Singapore's warm climate

4. Adult (Butterfly)

  • Emerges with wings
  • Feeds on nectar from flowers
  • Reproduces to start cycle again
  • Lives 2-4 weeks typically

🔹 Grasshopper Life Cycle (3 Stages)

1. Egg

  • Laid in soil or plant stems
  • Can survive dry seasons

2. Nymph

  • Looks like small adult but no wings
  • Eats plants, moults 5-6 times
  • Grows larger with each moult
  • Wing buds appear in later stages

3. Adult

  • Has fully developed wings
  • Can jump and fly
  • Reproduces to lay eggs
Key Difference: Nymphs look like mini-adults; caterpillars look nothing like butterflies!
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • Visit the Butterfly Park at Sentosa or Gardens by the Bay to observe butterfly life cycles in controlled environments.
  • Grasshoppers and cockroaches (incomplete metamorphosis) are common in Singapore's parks and HDB estates.
  • NParks' Butterfly Monitoring Programme tracks species like the Common Rose and Blue Tiger butterflies in nature reserves.
  • Mosquitoes (complete metamorphosis) breed in stagnant water — that's why Singapore's NEA promotes "Stop Dengue" campaigns to remove breeding sites.
💡Fun Fact: During the pupa stage, a caterpillar's body literally turns to soup before rebuilding into a butterfly! This amazing change is called complete metamorphosis.

🧊 States of Matter P4Diversity

MOE Learning Objective: Compare properties of solids, liquids and gases; understand changes of state when heated or cooled.

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe properties: solids (fixed shape/volume), liquids (fixed volume, no fixed shape), gases (no fixed shape/volume)
  • Explain melting, freezing, evaporation and condensation
  • Give examples of matter changing state in daily life in Singapore
  • Understand that matter is made of tiny particles that move differently in each state

🔹 Properties of the Three States

Solids

  • Have a fixed shape and fixed volume
  • Particles are tightly packed and vibrate in place
  • Examples: ice, book, chair, HDB brick

Liquids

  • Have a fixed volume but no fixed shape (takes shape of container)
  • Particles are close but can slide past each other
  • Examples: water, milk, teh tarik, rain

Gases

  • Have no fixed shape and no fixed volume (fills container)
  • Particles are far apart and move quickly in all directions
  • Examples: air, steam, carbon dioxide from soda
Particle Model: Imagine particles as tiny balls. In solids they're packed tight; in gases they zoom around freely!

🔹 Changes of State

Heating Changes

  • Melting: Solid → Liquid (e.g., ice cream melting in Singapore heat)
  • Evaporation: Liquid → Gas (e.g., puddles drying after rain)
  • Boiling: Rapid evaporation throughout liquid at boiling point (100°C for water)

Cooling Changes

  • Freezing: Liquid → Solid (e.g., making ice cubes in freezer)
  • Condensation: Gas → Liquid (e.g., water droplets on cold drink can)
Remember: Heating gives particles more energy to move; cooling takes energy away.
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • Condensation: In our humid climate, you see water droplets on cold drinks (like ice lemon tea) or bathroom mirrors after a shower.
  • Evaporation: Puddles disappear quickly after our afternoon thunderstorms due to high temperatures and humidity.
  • Freezing: Ice from the freezer melts rapidly at room temperature — that's why we use insulated bags for takeaways!
  • Water conservation: Understanding evaporation helps us appreciate why covering reservoirs reduces water loss.
💡Fun Fact: Water is the only common substance that is less dense as a solid (ice) than as a liquid — that's why ice floats! This protects aquatic life in cold climates.

1. Which statement about gases is TRUE? MCQ

2. What happens during condensation? MCQ

💧 The Water Cycle P5Cycles

MOE Learning Objective: Describe evaporation, condensation and precipitation in the water cycle; relate to Singapore's water management.

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain how the Sun's heat causes evaporation
  • Describe how water vapour condenses to form clouds
  • Understand how precipitation returns water to Earth
  • Relate the water cycle to Singapore's "Four National Taps" water strategy

🔹 The Water Cycle Steps

1. Evaporation

  • Sun heats water in oceans, lakes, rivers
  • Water changes from liquid to water vapour (gas)
  • Also happens from plants (transpiration)
  • Higher temperature + wind + low humidity = faster evaporation

2. Condensation

  • Water vapour rises and cools in the atmosphere
  • Vapour changes back to tiny liquid droplets
  • Droplets gather around dust particles to form clouds
  • In Singapore, clouds often form in the afternoon due to heating

3. Precipitation

  • Cloud droplets combine and grow heavy
  • Fall as rain, hail, or snow (rain in Singapore!)
  • Our average annual rainfall: ~2,340 mm

4. Collection

  • Rainwater flows into rivers, reservoirs, oceans
  • Some soaks into ground (infiltration)
  • Cycle repeats!

🔹 Singapore's Water Management

Singapore uses the water cycle principles in our "Four National Taps" strategy:

  • Local Catchment Water: Collecting rainwater in 17 reservoirs (e.g., Marina Reservoir, MacRitchie)
  • Imported Water: From Malaysia (under agreements)
  • NEWATER: High-grade reclaimed water using advanced treatment (reverse osmosis)
  • Desalinated Water: Removing salt from seawater at plants like Tuas
Water Conservation: Every drop counts! Singaporeans use ~150 litres/person/day — the goal is to reduce to 130 by 2030.
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • Marina Barrage: Creates a freshwater reservoir in the city while preventing floods — a brilliant engineering use of the water cycle!
  • ABC Waters Programme: Active, Beautiful, Clean waters — transforming drains and reservoirs into community spaces.
  • Afternoon thunderstorms: Common in Singapore due to intense heating causing rapid evaporation and cloud formation.
  • Water scarcity awareness: Though we get lots of rain, we have limited land for storage — that's why conservation matters.
💡Fun Fact: The water you drink today could be the same water a dinosaur drank millions of years ago! Water is constantly recycled through the water cycle.

⚡ Electrical Systems P5SystemsEnergy

MOE Learning Objective: Draw circuit diagrams; identify conductors/insulators; understand complete circuits and electrical safety.

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Draw simple circuit diagrams using standard symbols
  • Explain why a circuit must be complete for current to flow
  • Test materials to classify them as conductors or insulators
  • Describe safe use of electricity at home and in Singapore

🔹 What is an Electric Circuit?

An electric circuit is a complete path that allows electric current to flow from a power source, through components, and back.

  • Power source: Battery or mains electricity (provides energy)
  • Conducting path: Wires (usually copper) that carry current
  • Load: Device that uses electricity (bulb, motor, buzzer)
  • Switch: Controls whether circuit is open (off) or closed (on)
Key Rule: Current only flows if the circuit is COMPLETE (no breaks). A switch opens/closes the circuit.

🔹 Conductors and Insulators

Conductors

  • Materials that allow electric current to flow easily
  • Examples: copper, aluminium, iron, steel, graphite (pencil lead), salt water
  • Used for wires, plugs, circuit components

Insulators

  • Materials that do not allow electric current to flow easily
  • Examples: plastic, rubber, glass, wood, dry air, ceramic
  • Used for wire coatings, plug casings, switch covers for safety
Safety First: Never touch electrical appliances with wet hands — water (especially with impurities) can conduct electricity and cause shock!

🔹 Circuit Symbols (MOE Standard)

  • Battery: Long line (+) and short line (-)
  • Bulb: Circle with X inside
  • Switch (open): Break in line with angled line
  • Switch (closed): Continuous line
  • Wire: Straight line
Practice: Draw a circuit with 1 battery, 1 bulb, and 1 switch. Label each part!
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • Electrical safety: Singapore uses 230V AC at 50Hz. Always use 3-pin plugs with earth wire for metal appliances.
  • Energy conservation: SP Group encourages switching off lights/fans when not in use — every kWh saved helps our sustainability goals.
  • Renewable energy: Solar panels on HDB rooftops convert sunlight to electricity — a practical application of energy conversion!
  • Smart grids: Singapore is developing intelligent electricity networks to manage demand efficiently.
💡Fun Fact: Electricity travels at nearly the speed of light (~300,000 km/s)! When you flip a switch, the bulb lights up almost instantly.

1. Which material is a GOOD conductor of electricity? MCQ

2. What happens if a circuit has a break? MCQ

🎯 Forces P6Interactions

MOE Learning Objective: Identify types of forces; explain effects on motion; apply to real-world situations.

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define force as a push or pull
  • Identify: gravity, friction, magnetic force, elastic force
  • Explain how forces change motion, speed, direction, or shape
  • Predict effects of balanced vs unbalanced forces
  • Apply concepts to Singapore contexts

🔹 What is a Force?

A force is a push or pull that acts on an object. Forces can:

  • Make a stationary object start moving
  • Make a moving object stop, speed up, or slow down
  • Change the direction of a moving object
  • Change the shape of an object
Example: Kicking a football (push) makes it move. Catching it (pull) stops it.

🔹 Types of Forces

1. Gravitational Force (Gravity)

  • Pulling force towards Earth's centre
  • Gives objects weight; causes falling
  • Keeps Moon orbiting Earth

2. Frictional Force (Friction)

  • Opposes motion between surfaces
  • Acts opposite to direction of motion
  • Can be useful (walking) or disadvantage (wear)

3. Magnetic Force

  • Exerted by magnets on magnetic materials
  • Like poles repel; unlike poles attract
  • Can act without contact

4. Elastic Force

  • Exerted by stretched/compressed objects
  • Objects try to return to original shape
  • Used in springs, rubber bands

🔹 Balanced vs Unbalanced Forces

  • Balanced: Equal forces opposite directions → object stays still or moves at constant speed
  • Unbalanced: Unequal forces → object accelerates, decelerates, or changes direction
Tug-of-war: Equal pull = rope doesn't move (balanced). One team pulls harder = rope moves (unbalanced).
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • Friction: Grooved pedestrian crossings increase grip during rain.
  • Gravity: Water flows downhill through drainage systems.
  • Elastic force: Springs in school bag straps improve comfort.
  • Magnetic force: Compasses for orienteering at MacRitchie.
💡Fun Fact: Without friction, you couldn't walk — your feet would slip! Without gravity, everything would float into space.

🕸️ Food Webs & Interactions P6Interactions

MOE Learning Objective: Construct food chains/webs; identify producers/consumers/decomposers; explain population interactions.

🎯 By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Draw food chains using arrows to show energy flow
  • Identify producers (plants), consumers (animals), decomposers
  • Construct simple food webs showing multiple feeding relationships
  • Predict effects of removing one organism from a food web
  • Relate to conservation efforts in Singapore

🔹 Energy Flow in Ecosystems

All living things need energy to survive. In ecosystems, energy flows from the Sun through living things.

  • Producers: Plants/algae that make food via photosynthesis (use Sun's energy)
  • Consumers: Animals that eat other organisms
    • Primary consumers (herbivores): eat plants
    • Secondary consumers (carnivores): eat herbivores
    • Tertiary consumers: eat other carnivores
  • Decomposers: Bacteria/fungi that break down dead matter, returning nutrients to soil
Arrow Direction: In food chains, arrows point FROM food TO eater (showing energy flow). Example: Grass → Rabbit → Fox

🔹 Food Chains vs Food Webs

Food Chain

  • Single path of energy flow
  • Example: Leaves → Caterpillar → Bird → Hawk
  • Simple but unrealistic — most animals eat multiple things

Food Web

  • Multiple interconnected food chains
  • Shows complex feeding relationships
  • More realistic representation of ecosystems
  • If one species declines, others may be affected
Stability: Food webs are more stable than chains — if one food source disappears, animals may switch to another.

🔹 Population Interactions

Predator-Prey Relationships

  • When prey numbers increase, predators have more food → predator numbers increase
  • More predators eat more prey → prey numbers decrease
  • Fewer prey → predators struggle → predator numbers decrease
  • Cycle repeats (population oscillation)

Competition

  • Organisms compete for limited resources: food, water, space, mates
  • Can be within species or between species

Interdependence

  • All organisms in an ecosystem depend on each other
  • Changes to one population can affect many others
🇸🇬Singapore Context:
  • Bukit Timah Nature Reserve: Home to food webs connecting plants, insects, birds (e.g., Pink-necked Pigeon), mammals (e.g., Lesser Mousedeer).
  • NParks conservation: Protecting habitats ensures food web balance — e.g., preserving native plants supports native insects and birds.
  • Urban biodiversity: Even in HDB estates, food webs exist: grass → grasshopper → mynah → eagle.
  • Invasive species: Introduced species (e.g., Apple Snail) can disrupt local food webs by outcompeting natives.
💡Fun Fact: Only about 10% of energy passes from one level to the next in a food chain — that's why there are fewer top predators than plants!
